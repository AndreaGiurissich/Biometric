\title{Robustness of Fingerprint Recognition on SOCOFing:\\
A Comparison of Three Frozen Models under Increasing Alterations\\
and a Contrast-Mask Preprocessing Pipeline}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Andrea Giurissich}
\IEEEauthorblockA{Biometric Systems Project}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We present a closed-set 1:N biometric identification study on the
\textbf{SOCOFing} dataset~\cite{socofing} comparing three inference-only
(\emph{frozen}) recognition models --- a \textbf{Gabor} texture descriptor, a
\textbf{SIFT\,+\,RANSAC} keypoint matcher, and a \textbf{DINOv2} deep embedding
--- and quantifying how a contrast-mask preprocessing pipeline affects
performance as alteration difficulty increases (Easy/Medium/Hard) and across
alteration types (obliteration, central rotation, Z-cut). Every model is run on a
shared, seeded, stratified \num{2000}-probe manifest against the full
\num{6000}-identity gallery, and evaluated with both identification (Rank-$k$,
CMC, MRR) and verification (ROC, EER, AUC, FAR@FRR, FRR@FAR) metrics; the
baseline-vs-preprocessing effect is tested with paired statistics (McNemar and a
paired bootstrap). The headline result overturns a common intuition: the most
classical method (SIFT) is the most accurate and the most robust, degrading by
only $2.7$ points of Rank-1 from Easy to Hard; the recent deep embedding (frozen
DINOv2) is the weakest and \emph{collapses} (a $57$-point drop); and the
preprocessing benefit is \textbf{model-dependent}, clearly helping the texture
model, marginal for the deep model, and statistically neutral for the keypoint
matcher.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
fingerprint recognition, SOCOFing, SIFT, RANSAC, Gabor, DINOv2, minutiae,

\section{Introduction}
Fingerprint recognition is a mature biometric task, yet the relative merits of
classical hand-crafted representations and modern self-supervised deep features
are rarely compared \emph{under controlled degradation}. We use SOCOFing, whose
synthetic alterations provide graded, typed difficulty, to ask two questions:
(i)~how do a texture descriptor, a keypoint matcher, and a frozen deep embedding
rank in accuracy and robustness as alterations worsen; and (ii)~is a generic
contrast-enhancement preprocessing pipeline a universal benefit, or does its
value depend on the downstream representation? Both answers are, perhaps
surprisingly, counter to the ``deep-is-best, sharpening-always-helps'' intuition.

\section{The SOCOFing dataset}
\textbf{SOCOFing} (Sokoto Coventry Fingerprint Dataset)~\cite{socofing} contains
fingerprints from \num{600} subjects, \num{10} fingers each, for a total of
\textbf{\num{6000} identities} (an identity is a single finger, not a subject).
Each image is grayscale BMP at low resolution ($\sim\!96\times103$ px, i.e.\
$\sim\!\num{200}$\,dpi on disk). For each identity the dataset provides one
\textbf{Real} (unaltered) image, used as the \emph{gallery} template, and several
\textbf{Altered} images, used as \emph{probes}. The alterations were produced by
the SOCOFing authors with the STRANGE toolbox~\cite{socofing} in three severity
levels --- \emph{Easy}, \emph{Medium}, \emph{Hard} --- and three types:
\textbf{Obl} (obliteration: erasure/degradation of the ridge structure),
\textbf{CR} (central rotation of a portion), and \textbf{Zcut} (a Z-shaped cut
removing part of the image).

The protocol is \emph{closed-set 1:N identification}: the gallery is all
\num{6000} Real images (one template per identity); probes are the Altered
images, evaluated separately per level and per alteration type; by construction
every probe's true identity is present in the gallery.

\section{Recognition models}
All models are \textbf{frozen}: no training or fine-tuning is performed on
SOCOFing --- pure, deterministic inference. They share a unified
\texttt{extract}/\texttt{score} interface (Table~\ref{tab:models}), but differ
fundamentally in \emph{what} they represent (global vs local, texture vs geometry
vs learned semantics), which is precisely what the robustness comparison probes.

\subsection{Gabor texture descriptor}
A Gabor filter is a complex sinusoid modulated by a Gaussian envelope; it is
jointly selective in \emph{orientation} and \emph{spatial frequency} and is a
classical model of oriented ridge texture, underpinning the FingerCode
approach to fingerprint matching~\cite{jain2000}. We build a bank of
$8$ orientations $\theta_o=o\pi/8$ and $4$ frequencies (\num{32} filters). Each
image is resized to a canonical size (so that a frequency expressed in cycles/px
denotes the same physical scale across the variably-sized altered images), and
every filter is zero-meaned to remove its DC response so that flat regions
contribute nothing. For each filter we convolve the image and take the magnitude
of the response; the mean absolute response is then pooled over an $8\times8$
spatial grid. Concatenating over $32$ filters and $64$ cells yields a
\textbf{\num{2048}-dimensional} descriptor, L2-normalized. Two descriptors are
compared by \textbf{cosine similarity}. The representation is thus \emph{local}
(grid cells) and captures oriented ridge \emph{texture} statistics; it does not
model ridge geometry explicitly.

\subsection{SIFT + RANSAC}
The Scale-Invariant Feature Transform~\cite{lowe2004} detects keypoints as
scale-space extrema of a difference-of-Gaussian pyramid; each keypoint is
described by a $128$-dimensional histogram of local gradient orientations,
invariant to scale and rotation and robust to moderate affine and illumination
change. Matching between a probe and a gallery image proceeds in two stages.
First, descriptors are matched by approximate nearest neighbour (FLANN) and
filtered with \emph{Lowe's ratio test}~\cite{lowe2004} (ratio $0.75$), which
discards ambiguous matches whose best and second-best neighbours are too close.
Second, the surviving putative matches are subjected to \textbf{geometric
verification}: a homography is fitted with RANSAC~\cite{fischler1981}, and the
number of \textbf{geometrically consistent inliers} is used as the similarity
score. This two-stage design is the source of SIFT's robustness here: RANSAC
retains only the keypoints that agree on a single consistent transformation and
\emph{discards} those from occluded, rotated, or obliterated regions. The cost is
that scoring is $O(N\times\text{gallery})$: every probe is matched against all
\num{6000} gallery templates.

\subsection{DINOv2 embedding}
DINOv2~\cite{oquab2024} is a Vision Transformer (ViT-B/14)~\cite{dosovitskiy2021}
pretrained with a self-supervised objective on a large corpus of \emph{natural}
images, producing general-purpose visual features without labels. The image is
split into $14\times14$-pixel patches ($16\times16$ patches at $224\times224$),
each linearly embedded with a positional encoding; a special \texttt{[CLS]} token
is prepended, and the stacked self-attention layers let it aggregate a
\emph{global} image representation. We use the model frozen: the grayscale image
is resized to $224\times224$, replicated to three channels, ImageNet-normalized,
and passed through the network in eval/no-grad mode; the resulting $768$-D CLS
embedding is L2-normalized and compared by \textbf{cosine similarity}. Crucially,
the representation is \emph{global} and \emph{out-of-distribution} for
low-resolution fingerprint texture --- two properties that, as the results show,
make it fragile to local alterations.

\subsection{The dropped minutiae model: NBIS and MinutiaeNet}
\label{sec:nbis}
The study was originally designed with a fourth, \emph{minutiae}-based model ---
the canonical and forensically accepted fingerprint representation, in which an
identity is encoded by the set of ridge \emph{endings} and \emph{bifurcations}
(each a triplet of position and orientation, $x,y,\theta$). We report its
investigation in full because its \emph{failure} is itself informative about the
dataset.

Because no maintained Python binding of SourceAFIS exists, we used NIST's
\textbf{NBIS}~\cite{nbis}. Its extractor, \textbf{MINDTCT}, estimates the local
ridge \emph{flow} (orientation) and \emph{frequency} on a grid of image blocks,
binarizes and thins the ridges to a one-pixel skeleton, detects endings and
bifurcations on that skeleton, and assigns each a quality value, emitting a
minutiae template. The matcher, \textbf{BOZORTH3}, builds rotation- and
translation-invariant intra- and inter-fingerprint compatibility tables from two
templates and finds the largest mutually consistent cluster of minutiae pairs,
whose size is the match score.

On SOCOFing this pipeline is \emph{plumbing-correct but degenerate}. MINDTCT is
calibrated for $\sim\!\num{500}$\,dpi full-finger captures and partitions the
image into small ($\sim\!8$-px) blocks for ridge-flow and frequency estimation;
at SOCOFing's $\sim\!96\times103$ px there are too few valid blocks, so almost no
minutiae survive --- typically \textbf{4--6 per image} against the dozens a usable
print yields --- and BOZORTH3, unable to reach its minimum matching-pair count,
returns \emph{zero} scores. We then tried a deep, resolution-tolerant extractor,
\textbf{MinutiaeNet}~\cite{nguyen2018} (a CoarseNet\,+\,FineNet CNN, frozen). At
native resolution it extracted essentially \emph{no} minutiae; only after
$\sim\!4\times$ upsampling did it recover $\sim\!15$--$20$ minutiae, and even then
the yield was noisy and non-monotonic in the upscale factor and validated on a
single fingerprint. Because this fragile gain would have required an
upsampling step (a change to the frozen preprocessing) for a template far weaker
than the other models', the \textbf{minutiae paradigm was dropped}, and the study
proceeds with three models. The finding stands on its own: \emph{SOCOFing's native
resolution is below the operating point of both classical and deep minutiae
extractors}.

\subsection{Contrast-mask preprocessing}
The preprocessing under test is a \textbf{contrast-mask} operator adapted from
face-detection robustness work (no facial alignment). A soft mask of high-contrast
regions is built (Gaussian denoise $\to$ Laplacian $\to$ blur $\to$ threshold
$\to$ blur), the original is sharpened by \emph{unsharp masking}~\cite{gonzalez},
and the two are blended according to the mask,
$\text{out}=m\cdot\text{sharp}+(1-m)\cdot\text{orig}$, so that ridge regions are
sharpened while the background is left intact. In the \texttt{preprocessed}
condition the operator is applied \textbf{symmetrically to gallery and probes}
with identical parameters; the \texttt{baseline} condition uses raw images.

\section{Experimental protocol}
\subsection{Conditions and evaluation tier}
Each model is evaluated under two conditions (\emph{baseline} vs
\emph{preprocessed}) on \textbf{Tier A}: a \num{2000}-probe manifest, stratified
by alteration type and seeded, shared across models for a fair comparison. (A
Tier-B ``best estimate'' over the full probe set is used for the embedding models;
SIFT's $O(N\times\text{gallery})$ cost makes a full-set run infeasible, so SIFT is
reported at Tier~A.)

\subsection{Metrics}
For \textbf{identification} (rank-based, comparable across models) we report
Rank-1/5/10, the full CMC curve, and MRR~\cite{maltoni}; the rank of the true
mate is computed with pessimistic tie handling. For \textbf{verification} (each
model's native, non-normalized score) we report the ROC, EER, AUC --- computed
exactly as the Mann--Whitney statistic~\cite{hanley1982} to avoid trapezoidal
tie artifacts --- and the operating points FAR@FRR=1\% and
FRR@FAR=1\%~\cite{maltoni,cappelli2006}. There is one genuine score per probe;
impostors are \num{100} seeded non-self identities per probe.

\subsection{Statistical significance}
Because both conditions are evaluated on the same probes, the comparison is
\emph{paired}: McNemar's test~\cite{mcnemar1947} on Rank-1 hits, and a paired
bootstrap~\cite{efron1979} (resampling probes) for the confidence interval and
$p$-value of $\Delta$EER and $\Delta$AUC, both overall and per alteration type.

\subsection{Implementation}
The code is config-driven (a single YAML source of truth, all randomness seeded).
A model-agnostic pipeline extracts the \num{6000} gallery descriptors once (with a
parameter-signed cache), then for each probe extracts its descriptor, scores it
against the whole gallery, and records the true-mate rank and the
genuine/impostor scores incrementally (resume-safe). Execution runs on Kaggle (a
T4 GPU for DINOv2; SIFT is CPU-bound and process-parallel). All randomness
(subsampling, impostor sampling, RANSAC) is seeded, and raw per-probe scores are
archived with a snapshot of the configuration and library versions.

\section{Results}
SIFT numbers are computed directly from the archived per-probe scores in
\texttt{results/} (\num{2000} probes per cell); Gabor and DINOv2 numbers are from
the same Tier-A, \num{2000}-probe protocol.

\subsection{Identification: ranking and robustness}
The ranking is \textbf{SIFT $\gg$ Gabor $\gg$ DINOv2} at every level
(Table~\ref{tab:rank1}, Fig.~\ref{fig:rank1}), and the gap \emph{widens} with
difficulty: at Hard, SIFT reaches $96.9\%$ Rank-1 versus DINOv2's $28.3\%$. SIFT
barely degrades ($-2.7$ points Easy$\to$Hard) while DINOv2 \textbf{collapses}
($-57$ points). The mechanism is structural: SIFT's RANSAC step keeps only the
\emph{surviving} consistent keypoints and ignores the rest, tolerating local
damage, whereas DINOv2's single global embedding shifts whenever the image
changes and is out-of-distribution on fingerprint texture.

\subsection{Verification: ROC and operating points}
The verification picture is even sharper (Table~\ref{tab:verif},
Figs.~\ref{fig:roc}--\ref{fig:eer}). SIFT's inlier-count score separates genuine
from impostor comparisons by a wide margin (mean $\sim\!30$ inliers vs
$\sim\!0.5$), giving EER around $0.3$--$1.2\%$ and, critically, a usable
\textbf{strict operating point}: at FRR=1\% its FAR stays at $0.06$--$1.5\%$
across all levels. Gabor and DINOv2, despite respectable EER/AUC, have heavily
overlapping score tails: at FRR=1\% their FAR exceeds $60$--$77\%$ on the harder
levels, i.e.\ they are \emph{unusable} at a low-false-reject threshold. The SIFT
ROC (Fig.~\ref{fig:roc}) hugs the top-left corner even at Hard (AUC $0.998$),
while the EER trend (Fig.~\ref{fig:eer}) shows the three models diverging with
difficulty.

\subsection{Per-alteration analysis}
The per-type breakdown (Table~\ref{tab:alt}) reveals the failure modes.
\textbf{DINOv2 fails on all} hard alterations --- even on a clean cut (Zcut,
$0.305$) that SIFT and Gabor absorb --- because a global embedding cannot tolerate
a missing region. \textbf{Gabor's weakness is obliteration} ($0.654$): texture
statistics degrade when ridges are destroyed, though it stays robust to cutting
($0.981$). \textbf{SIFT is robust to all types} ($\ge0.954$): geometric keypoint
consistency survives rotation, cutting, and partial obliteration alike.

\subsection{Effect of preprocessing}
The most nuanced result (Table~\ref{tab:prep}, Fig.~\ref{fig:prep}): the contrast
mask \textbf{helps the texture descriptor (Gabor), increasingly with difficulty}
($+0.7$/$+3.0$/$+3.4$ points); is \textbf{marginal for the deep embedding} (DINOv2,
$\sim\!+0.6$ flat); and is \textbf{statistically neutral for the keypoint matcher
(SIFT)} --- the paired McNemar test is non-significant at every level (Easy $p=0.69$,
Medium $p=0.23$, Hard $p=0.15$) and the $\Delta$EER bootstrap intervals include
zero. Indeed sharpening slightly \emph{hurts} SIFT at Hard, plausibly because it
introduces spurious keypoints that break geometric consistency. A preprocessing
operator devised for facial robustness is therefore \emph{not} a universal good:
its value is contingent on the downstream representation.

\section{Discussion}
The study overturns common intuition twice. First, the most recent deep model
(frozen DINOv2) is the \emph{worst} and collapses on hard alterations, while the
oldest method (SIFT\,+\,RANSAC) is the \emph{best} and barely degrades: on altered
fingerprints, \emph{local, geometric} robustness beats the global semantics of an
out-of-distribution embedding. Second, a preprocessing pipeline that helps one
model (Gabor) is neutral or harmful for another (SIFT), so preprocessing benefit
is \emph{contingent on the representation}, not universal. The per-alteration
analysis makes the mechanisms concrete: global embeddings are fragile to cuts,
texture statistics to obliteration, and geometric keypoint matching to none of
them.

\paragraph{Limitations}
SIFT is reported at Tier A (\num{2000} probes); its $O(N\times\text{gallery})$
cost makes a full-probe Tier-B run infeasible. DINOv2 is used exactly as its
frozen specification dictates (CLS token, $224$ px), so the result reflects
off-the-shelf features rather than a higher-resolution or fine-tuned variant.
Paired significance and full ROC/FRR@FAR are computed for SIFT, whose per-probe
scores are archived; for Gabor/DINOv2 the reported operating points are from the
same protocol and their paired tests can be reproduced with the provided tooling
once their per-probe scores are re-archived.

\section{Conclusions}
On SOCOFing, a classical SIFT\,+\,RANSAC matcher is by a wide margin the most
accurate and robust fingerprint recognizer among the three, the only one usable
at a strict security threshold, and essentially insensitive to the tested
preprocessing; a Gabor texture descriptor is intermediate and benefits from
preprocessing increasingly with difficulty; and a frozen DINOv2 embedding is the
weakest and collapses under hard alterations. The practical takeaways are that
geometric local matching is the safer choice for altered low-resolution prints,
and that contrast-enhancement preprocessing should be validated per model rather
than assumed beneficial.
